The Multi-Cloud GTM Playbook: AWS, Azure, and GCP in One Coordinated Motion

By Jen Dawson March 2026 11 min read AWS · Azure · GCP

Most multi-cloud GTM approaches fail because ISVs treat each cloud as a separate program with separate teams, separate KPIs, and separate board reporting. This creates three times the operational overhead with none of the compounding benefit. There is a more effective structure, and it starts with treating the hyperscaler ecosystem as a single motion with three execution points.

Why Multi-Cloud GTM Stalls

The challenge ISVs face when expanding from one cloud to two or three is not technical, it is organizational. Each cloud marketplace has its own co-sell program mechanics, field team structure, and partner portal. Most ISVs assume this means three separate GTM programs, three separate alliance managers, and three separate reporting streams to the board.

That assumption produces a predictable result: the first cloud gets the majority of resource and attention, the second gets intermittent effort, and the third either gets dropped entirely or receives only a listing with no co-sell execution. Boards see fragmented, inconsistent activity across clouds and draw the logical conclusion that marketplace is not a reliable revenue channel.

The underlying error is treating multi-cloud as a scaling challenge when it is actually a coordination challenge. The three cloud programs, AWS ACE, Azure co-sell, and GCP Partner Advantage, run on different mechanics but share identical underlying logic: field teams respond to ISVs who are credible, relevant, and specific in their asks. That logic does not change per cloud. The execution layer that responds to it does.

The Unified Multi-Cloud Framework

A coordinated multi-cloud GTM motion operates from a single activation plan with three execution tracks, one per cloud, coordinated by shared customer intelligence, a unified field relationship strategy, and a single board reporting format.

Building the Shared Customer Intelligence Layer

The first step in a coordinated multi-cloud GTM motion is building an ideal customer profile that applies across all three cloud programs. This is not three separate ICPs, it is one ICP with cloud-specific consumption indicators appended.

For example: a security ISV targeting mid-market financial services firms would identify the general buyer characteristics and then note which cloud services their target customers are most likely to be running on each cloud. AWS workloads in financial services skew toward data and analytics. Azure workloads often center on identity and compliance. GCP workloads increasingly appear in AI and data science functions. The same company may have footprints across all three, which means a single co-sell motion across all three can produce concurrent field engagement with the same prospect from three separate cloud partner directions.

That convergence, where multiple cloud field teams are simultaneously engaged on the same account, is one of the most powerful advantages of a coordinated multi-cloud approach. It is not possible when each cloud is run as a separate program with no shared customer intelligence.

The Per-Cloud Execution Mechanics

While the customer intelligence layer is shared, the execution mechanics on each cloud differ and must be respected. Applying an AWS ACE submission format directly to Azure co-sell or GCP Partner Advantage will produce poor results because the field team expectations and program requirements are different.

On AWS, the ACE program rewards specificity about AWS service involvement, cloud consumption impact, and the specific PDM relationship. WinWires are a primary trust signal and should be filed for every closed win that involved AWS services, regardless of how small the deal was.

On Azure, the co-sell program prioritizes IP co-sell status and deal registration. Microsoft field reps respond to ISVs who have achieved co-sell ready or Azure IP co-sell status, who register deals formally in the co-sell portal, and who engage in joint account planning with Microsoft sellers. The relationship dynamic is more structured than AWS and more responsive to formal program enrollment.

On GCP, the Partner Advantage program rewards ISVs who engage with Google Cloud field teams through formal enablement, who position their product as extending GCP-native services, and who participate in the Google Cloud Marketplace go-to-market programs. GCP field teams respond well to ISVs who can articulate specific data and AI consumption scenarios where their product extends what GCP already provides.

Unified Field Enablement Across Clouds

The most time-efficient component of a multi-cloud GTM motion is field enablement. A well-structured field enablement session, adapted for each cloud's field team context but built from the same core product and customer narrative, can run once per quarter per cloud with significant pipeline impact.

The session should cover three things: what the ISV's product does in plain business language, which customer profiles and use cases the field team should look for when co-selling, and what the specific co-sell ask is. That last element, the specific ask, is often missing from ISV-led enablement sessions and is the primary reason field teams leave without follow-through.

Producing a Single Board Report Across All Clouds

The defining output of a coordinated multi-cloud GTM motion is a single board pipeline report that consolidates co-sell activity, submissions, WinWires, and attributed revenue across AWS, Azure, and GCP into one document. This is the board-visible proof that cloud marketplace investment is producing a return, not on one cloud, but on the full hyperscaler ecosystem the ISV has invested in.

The format matters. A credible board pipeline report includes: the number of co-sell opportunities submitted per cloud and their current stage, the number of WinWires filed and their documented cloud involvement, field team relationship counts per cloud with named contacts, and a revenue attribution summary showing deals where cloud co-sell activity influenced the outcome. These elements can be produced from structured tracking of co-sell submissions across all three portals and do not require custom tooling.

The Execution Takeaway

Multi-cloud GTM does not require three times the effort if it is architected as a coordinated motion. Shared customer intelligence, per-cloud execution mechanics, unified field enablement, and a single board report format, these four components produce a multi-cloud GTM output that is more powerful than any single-cloud approach and more efficient than three separate programs.

The critical constraint is execution discipline: the co-sell submissions must actually be filed, the WinWires must actually be written, and the field enablement sessions must actually run. Frameworks without execution produce nothing. In cloud marketplace GTM, execution is the only variable that determines whether the investment produces board-visible pipeline.
