The July 31 AWS PRM Deadline: How to Measure Attribution, Keep Funding, and Avoid Co-Sell Delays

Jen Dawson
April 2026 5 min read

Visibility is the new currency inside AWS.

By July 31, 2026, AWS will measure partner impact through Partner Revenue Measurement (PRM), a system that connects your product's consumption directly to AWS's internal scorecards.

This isn't just a compliance date; it's the moment AWS decides which partners stay visible and funded. If your PRM data isn't live, your co-sell engine loses visibility. AWS sellers can't see your impact, your product stops showing up in internal scorecards, and your opportunities stop getting prioritized.

PRM isn't paperwork. It's how AWS sees you.

And for most partners, it's the most important compliance milestone they haven't been briefed on yet.

Miss the deadline and the consequences are immediate:

  • Loss of AWS Partner Funding Benefits (MDF/SDF)
  • Removal of the "Deployed on AWS" badge
  • Reduced co-sell eligibility with AWS Field teams
  • Freezing of your Marketplace and ACE motions

What PRM actually measures

PRM moves AWS from estimated partner influence to real service-level attribution.

It tracks consumption across EC2, S3, ECS, RDS, Lambda, and more, creating a transparent, data-backed signal that tells AWS: this partner drives usage that matters.

AWS attributes revenue through three signals:

  • Zero-touch AWS Marketplace Metering: Automatic attribution for AMI and ML products
  • Resource Tagging: Applying the aws-apn-id tag to cost-incurring AWS resources
  • API Call Decoration: Embedding User Agent strings in your regular AWS API/CLI calls

Why it matters

Partners with active PRM data show up differently on AWS scorecards.

Field teams can see consumption tied to your product, strengthening co-sell alignment and giving Partner Development Managers the metrics they need to advocate for you internally.

Without PRM, AWS can't see your impact, and you lose visibility in every motion that drives growth.

Avoid the DIY trap

Manual tagging and console-based fixes often break infrastructure and drain engineering time.

Common pitfalls include IaC drift, untagged shadow resources, and compliance drops when new workloads spin up unmonitored.
