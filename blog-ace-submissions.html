Why Your ACE Submissions Go Unanswered, and How to Fix That

By Jen Dawson March 2026 9 min read AWS · Azure · GCP

This post addresses a problem I have seen in nearly every ISV engagement across AWS, Azure, and GCP: co-sell opportunities that get submitted but never responded to. The causes are structural, and the fixes are executable.

The Submission That Goes Nowhere

An ISV alliance leader spends several hours building an ACE co-sell opportunity on AWS Marketplace. They research the deal, write a description, set a dollar value, and submit. Then they wait. A week passes. Two weeks. A follow-up email gets no reply. The PDM has moved on.

The same scenario plays out on Azure co-sell and GCP Partner Advantage. Submissions filed, field teams silent. The ISV concludes that cloud co-sell "doesn't work for us," pulls back from the program, and returns to direct sales only.

The problem is not the cloud program. The problem is how the submission was built.

Why Cloud Field Teams Ignore Most Submissions

Cloud partner managers, AWS PDMs, Microsoft sales specialists, Google Cloud field reps, operate under significant workload pressure. Each manages a large portfolio of ISV relationships. When a co-sell submission arrives, they make a rapid evaluation: is this deal relevant to my customers, does this ISV appear credible, and is there enough context to act on this immediately?

Most co-sell submissions fail that evaluation within the first 15 seconds. Here is why.

Problem 1: Generic deal descriptions

The most common submission error is a deal description written from the ISV's perspective rather than the cloud partner's. Phrases like "customer evaluating our security platform" or "prospect in Q3 pipeline" communicate nothing that would make a cloud field rep want to engage. They don't know the customer's use case, the relevant cloud services involved, or why this ISV specifically is a fit for this customer's situation.

Problem 2: No cloud-specific business context

AWS PDMs care about deals that involve or extend AWS services. Azure co-sell reps care about workloads running on Azure. GCP field teams respond to deals anchored in Google Cloud consumption. A generic ISV pitch with no cloud-native context reads as a sponsorship request, not a co-sell opportunity. It will be deprioritized accordingly.

Problem 3: Insufficient relationship capital

A co-sell submission from an ISV the field rep has never heard of, met, or collaborated with is starting from zero credibility. Cloud field teams route submissions from familiar ISV partners, ones who have run enablement sessions with them, submitted prior WinWires, and engaged in regular field communication, significantly faster than submissions from unknown entities.

Problem 4: Wrong opportunity stage

Submitting a deal that is too early in the sales cycle, or already closed, produces no co-sell value for either party. Cloud field reps are looking for mid-funnel opportunities where their engagement can actually influence the outcome, not informational submissions or retrospective filings.

The Submission Framework That Gets Responses

After working with 100+ ISVs across AWS, Azure, and GCP, SaaSNova has developed a submission framework that consistently generates field team engagement. It applies across all three cloud platforms, the mechanics differ slightly by program, but the underlying logic is identical.

01
Customer Context
Industry, size, cloud services in use, specific business problem
02
Cloud Fit
Why this deal extends or deepens cloud service consumption
03
Ask
One specific, actionable ask, introduction, technical review, event, or reference
04
Deal Stage
Current funnel stage, qualified, proposal, or evaluation, with a close timeline

Each of these four components changes how a cloud field partner reads and acts on the submission. Together, they shift the submission from a one-way broadcast into a collaboration request with specific, answerable content.

The Role of WinWires in Building Submission Credibility

A single strong co-sell submission does not build field trust on its own. What accelerates response rates significantly is a recent WinWire, a documented, submitted record of an ISV win that involved a cloud partner. WinWires create a track record in the cloud partner's ecosystem. They signal that the ISV is not just submitting opportunities but also closing them and attributing cloud platform value in the outcome.

ISVs who maintain a cadence of WinWires alongside co-sell submissions see substantially higher field team engagement. The field rep is not responding to a cold submission, they are responding to a known, proven ISV partner with a record of shared wins.

Field Enablement as Submission Infrastructure

The fastest route to a co-sell response is to have done an enablement session with the relevant field team before the submission arrives. When a cloud field rep has sat in a session where the ISV explained their product, identified target customer profiles, and ran through competitive differentiation, a subsequent co-sell submission from that ISV reads as a collaboration rather than a request for help.

Two field enablement sessions per quarter, structured correctly, transform the field team's relationship with the ISV from unfamiliar to trusted. Submissions from trusted ISVs get answered. Submissions from unfamiliar ISVs get queued indefinitely.

This Framework Applies Across All Three Clouds

The AWS ACE submission process, Azure co-sell workflow, and GCP Partner Advantage all operate on the same underlying logic: field teams respond to ISVs who demonstrate relevance, credibility, and a specific ask. The portal interfaces differ. The program names differ. The response mechanics are identical.

ISVs running co-sell across multiple clouds can operate a single submission framework with cloud-specific customizations, adapting the cloud consumption angle and program mechanics per cloud while maintaining the same customer context, ask structure, and deal stage format.

The Execution Takeaway

Most co-sell submissions go unanswered not because the cloud program is broken or because the ISV's product is the wrong fit. They go unanswered because the submission was built to document an opportunity, not to activate a field partner.

Rebuilding the submission framework, with specific customer context, clear cloud fit, a single actionable ask, and the right deal stage, is executable immediately. Adding a WinWire cadence and biannual field enablement sessions creates the structural relationship capital that makes future submissions faster and more reliable to respond.

This is not strategy. It is execution. And it produces measurable results within the first program period, on AWS, Azure, and GCP.
